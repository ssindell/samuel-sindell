An open letter to serious investors

I came for the trajectory, and stayed to build on it.

I had been coming to the UAE for more than ten years. Long enough to watch a city move with a clarity of intent I was not seeing elsewhere: a clear agenda, real growth, low friction. I wanted to be part of that, not observe it from outside.

Before Dubai I spent a decade in financial services. I led business development for a software consultancy and won the trust of firms including HSBC, BNY Mellon, Deutsche Bank and Gazprom, at the front edge of the move from machine learning into artificial intelligence. I learned how the top tier of global capital actually moves, and how rarely ordinary investors are shown the same playbook.

That is the work now. I advise private investors on acquiring Dubai real estate and on holding it efficiently across borders. The standard behind all of it is simple: I invest the same way I advise, and I only recommend what I would enter myself.

How I see the market

A measured position, not a market call.

The precision approach

Most people approach Dubai property as a single market you simply buy into. The reality is narrower. You have to align with the city's specific growth trajectory and find the point where location, infrastructure and developer all stack up. Get that intersection right and the result follows. Ignore it and you are buying postcodes.

On the "bubble" question

You will hear that Dubai is a bubble. It does not survive scrutiny. This is a Tier 1 city priced at roughly a third of London, Paris or New York. Real bubbles form where the fundamentals are broken: no growth agenda, no tourism, unstable rates, a GDP that is not working. Dubai has the opposite of all four.

Scarcity is mispriced

Under 6% of the market is waterfront and less than 1% has beach access, against more than 65% apartments and under 15% villas and townhouses. Scarce asset types in a growing market are where the right allocations sit.

The standard

The exit matters as much as the entry.

I have walked away from deals I could easily have closed, because I did not believe in the project. I will not sell on hype, a low price point, or a distressed-deal story. Everything I recommend has to stand on data with a clear exit thesis. I would rather lose the deal than sell something I would not enter myself.

What I walk away from:

  • A price per square foot too high to leave room for future growth
  • A weak or unproven developer track record
  • Projects that do not align with the government agendas driving the next growth phase
  • Anything sold on hype rather than evidence

Don't rush. Learn how, where, why and who, then act with precision.
